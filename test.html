Sunday, November 5, 2023
All Saints Sunday, Year A

Introduction

All Saints celebrates the baptized people of God, living and dead, who are the body of Christ. As November heralds the dying of the landscape in many northern regions, the readings and liturgy call us to remember all who have died in Christ and whose baptism is complete. At the Lord’s table we gather with the faithful of every time and place, trusting that the promises of God will be fulfilled and that all tears will be wiped away in the new Jerusalem.

Upcoming Commemorations

All Saints Day
Wednesday, November 1, 2023
The custom of commemorating all of the saints of the church on a single day goes back at least to the third century. All Saints Day celebrates the baptized people of God, living and dead, who make up the body of Christ. On this day or the following Sunday, many congregations will remember the faithful who have died during the past year.
Martín de Porres, renewer of society, died 1639
Friday, November 3, 2023
Martín was the son of a Spanish knight and a freed Black woman from Panama. As a lay brother in the Order of Preachers (Dominicans), he engaged in many charitable works in Lima, Peru. He founded an orphanage, a hospital, and a clinic for cats and dogs.
John Christian Frederick Heyer, died 1873; Bartholomaeus Ziegenbalg, died 1719; Ludwig Nommensen, died 1918; missionaries
Tuesday, November 7, 2023
Heyer was the first missionary sent out by American Lutherans. After teaching at Gettysburg College and Seminary he was assigned to the Andhra region of India. A worker among the Tamil people on the southeast coast of India, Ziegenbalg was opposed both by local Hindus and by Danish authorities who favored a different missionary style. He was known for caring about the welfare of the whole person. Nommensen was born in northern Germany and was sent to Sumatra as a Lutheran missionary. Working among the Batak people, he translated the scriptures into their language, and honored their native culture.
Martin, Bishop of Tours, died 397
Saturday, November 11, 2023
Martin's pagan father enlisted him in the Roman army. Approached by a beggar, Martin cut his cloak in half and gave part to the man, whom he later understood as representing Christ. He asked to leave the army, but was jailed instead. After his release, he became a preacher, and later a bishop.
Søren Aabye Kierkegaard, teacher, died 1855
Saturday, November 11, 2023
Kierkegaard was a Danish theologian whose writings also gave rise to the modern philosophy of existentialism. He frequently attacked the complacency of the state church of his day, as well as its desire to be accepted by polite society rather than be a stumbling block.

Sunday, November 5, 2023
All Saints Sunday

Overview

Saintly Sinners, Sinful Saints

All Saints Sunday and the appointed readings are pregnant with themes of stark contrast: death and life, condemnation and redemption, sorrow and joy, suffering and relief, present life and future life. Such is life, too, as baptized children of God. The very nature of our lives is of stark contrast.

By virtue of our baptism into the death and resurrection of Jesus, we are made saints while we are still sinners. Every day in this nautically juxtaposed life we navigate through rough waters such as sin, evil, pain, heartache, addiction, abuse, hunger, disease, and war. But our bearing is always set, not by the North Star but by the Daystar, who has been the heading for the faithful of every generation. With the scriptures as our compass and the confessions our map, we sail as a part of the great flotilla of saints on the waters of baptism, with the wind of the Spirit at our backs, always pointed in the direction of our guiding Daystar.

Today we give thanks for the saints who have gone before us, for the saints still among us, and for the saints of God still to come. We give thanks for the examples of faithful living that the saints have given us as they navigated the stormy waters of life, and also for the faithful acts of service we are empowered by God’s Holy Spirit to do in our own seas. We give thanks, too, for the fullness of eternal life in which the saints of God who have gone before us now partake and which we have yet to fully enjoy.

Ideas

Current Event / Pop Culture

Cemeteries house the dead, but they are for the living. One way to discover how a community or even an entire culture interacts with death is through the sacred tradition of commemorating the saints. In Slovakia, All Saints Day is a national holiday as well as a religious one. Slovak cemeteries come alive as people adorn their loved ones’ tombs with candles, flowers, and other local trinkets. It is typical to see ancient water basins throughout the graveyard, available for visitors to wash tombstones. All Saints Day is also a family affair where all generations learn about their heritage while facing their own mortality.

Pastoral Care / Testimony

It is common for people to ignore the realities of death out of fear. What are some ways clergy can engage in this difficult topic? Perhaps host a discussion on funeral planning and explore what it means to “believe in the resurrection of the body and the life everlasting.” Reach out to local funeral directors to see if they would present a forum to your congregation on preparing for death. Martin Luther believed it was necessary for every Christian to prepare for their death in order to put families at ease and quell any internal disputes regarding estates and money.

Theological Reflection Illustration

All Saints Day is when the universal church—past, present, and future—gathers to rejoice in the one body of Christ. Think of all the people that entails! John describes this in Revelation as “a great multitude that no one could count, from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages, standing before the throne” (Rev. 7:9). How do we help others recognize their sainthood as well as their part in the salvation story? There are famous and not-so-famous saints, but it is important to emphasize that sainthood through baptism is the great equalizer, for as 1 John says: “See what love the Father has given us, that we should be called children of God; and that is what we are” (3:1). This day could inspire a message that in holy communion we are in the presence of Christ and of all the saints of every time and place.

Book

Talking about death at any age is hard, but how does one talk about it with a child? Christians live with “daily dying”; at funerals we are ideally focused more on baptism than on all the person’s life achievements. Christians gather around a man dying on a cross, proclaiming his crucifixion as gospel (1 Corinthians 2:1-2). A big part of the Christian message is death. Consider hosting a mini-retreat for young people who have experienced grief in some fashion (moving, divorce, or family death). In his book Remembering Crystal, Sebastian Loth gently explains to children the grief process (New York: North-South Books, 2010).

Song

One of the suggested hymns for today is “In our day of thanksgiving” (ELW 429). This is an excellent hymn to include in the All Saints Day or Sunday’s service. Can the day to commemorate the dead be called a day of thanksgiving? Yes, when we examine its message: “. . . one psalm let us offer for the saints who before us received the reward; when the shadow of death fell upon them, we sorrowed, but now we rejoice that they rest in the Lord.” Perhaps useful for a eucharistic connection, highlight: “In the morning of life, and at noon, and at evening, they were gathered to heaven from our worship below; but not till God’s love, at the font and the altar, had clothed them with grace for the way they should go.”

Visual Image / Song

Every night someone somewhere sings or says the Nunc dimittis, Simeon’s Song from Luke 2:29-32. Fitting for the last prayer service of the day (compline), this canticle is often used at funerals. Sing a version of Simeon’s Song in worship (see ELW, p. 324). Arent de Gelder’s painting Simeon’s Song of Praise is a visual interpretation of Simeon’s Song. While it may seem more appropriate for Christmas/Epiphany, the painting depicts Simeon, a faithful old man who has been eagerly waiting for the Savior, holding the baby Jesus. With Advent approaching, this painting may give viewers a “full-circle” look at the liturgical calendar.

Book

In his pivotal book The Mission Table: Renewing Congregations and Community (Minneapolis: Augsburg Fortress, 2013), former bishop Stephen Bouman uses table imagery to explore ways the church can be relevant in community by connecting the altar with the kitchen table. He opens with a salient theme. A Korean family buries their family’s patriarch and after the committal, have a large, festive picnic lunch—right alongside the tombstones. Bouman concludes:”. . . eating and drinking with the Lee family was sacramental. . . . In the graveyard, Mr. Lee’s family set a table.”

Let the Children Come

On All Saints Sunday we remember the great cloud of witnesses who have gone before us on the journey through life. Children may find this day fascinating as we remember loved ones with lit candles, special prayers, and liturgies. Children themselves may have experienced the loss of a family member, or perhaps a pet, and may want to remember them. Today’s reading from Revelation pictures the multitude who have gone before us singing praise to God. Children can join their voices to these songs (“Holy, holy, holy” or “This is the feast”) to remember and reunite with those loved ones.

Connections with Creation

All Saints celebrates equality among the saints, especially remembering those who have died and returned to the earth. Theologian Dorothee Soelle wonders if the equality we experience in death inspires revolutionary movements for equality among the living. She writes in The Mystery of Death: “I often ask myself whether death is not the very inventor of equality and whether human beings are permitted any equality at all without the consciousness of death’s power. As [death] is repressed in the world of the rich, so too the consciousness of human equality disappears. . . . Can equality in the negative sense be so neatly distinguished from equality in the positive, revolutionary sense of human rights for all?” (Fortress Press, 1984, p. 10). Could it be that the communion of saints and the earth itself work together to inspire revolutionary movements for equality and justice?

Lectionary Notes by Gail Ramshaw

All Saints, Year A

Gail Ramshaw

E-formation

On All Saints Day, we thank God for all the baptized, both the famous and the forgotten, both those who lived in the past and those around us today. Come to worship, to gather at Jesus’ feet and to receive his words of blessing.

The Readings in the Bible

Matthew 5:1-12
It seems that in the mid-80s the author of the gospel of Matthew compiled smaller units of the sayings of Jesus into what we call the Sermon on the Mount, a single lengthy address meant to parallel the primary address of Moses from Mount Sinai. The word translated “blessed” can also be translated “happy.” The Beatitudes indicate Matthew’s sense of the transformed values inherent within the kingdom of heaven. This kingdom, of central importance in this gospel, is not here defined, but it honors persons not normally revered in society. The Beatitudes assume that persecution of believers is imminent.

Revelation 7:9-17
Probably written in the late first century or early second century, Revelation names John as its author, who never claims to be the John of the original Jesus movement. The author employs the apocalyptic style and imagery of Daniel and intertestamental writings in his visions of the evil power of the Roman Empire and the salvation that Jesus Christ will bring at the end of all things. Typical of those social situations that inspire apocalyptic thinking, Revelation sees no way out of the current sufferings except for divine intervention and the consequent destruction of evil. The excerpt describes a vision of heaven in which the saved represent all peoples on earth who have suffered for their faith but now experience happiness.

1 John 3:1-3
Anonymously written in the late first or early second century perhaps in Ephesus, 1 John evidences a community familiar with the theological style of the Gospel of John. Not actually a letter, the essay declares the truth of the community’s faith in the sacrificial death of Jesus Christ, warns the community about false teachings, and calls the community to mutual love. The excerpt states that the children of God, those who know and are known by God, are not those known by the world.

The Readings on This Day

All Saints
The custom of commemorating the saints of the church, especially martyrs, on a single day originated in the fourth century. By the eighth century, the festival commemorating all saints had been moved in England and Ireland to November 1 in order to Christianize the Celtic harvest festival of Samhain which marked the beginning of winter and communal attention to the dead. In current Christian use, the day is dedicated to thanksgiving for all the unnamed who died in the Lord. In many assemblies, the names of the local dead are read aloud, a focus originally associated with All Souls Day on November 2. The conflation of the two emphases fits with the Protestant belief that the all the Christian dead are saints and none needs release from purgatory.

The readings for All Saints are best used at a service scheduled on November 1. Some assemblies observe All Saints on the first Sunday in November. Current usage suggests instead to maintain the usual Sunday readings but to add an emphasis on all saints, for example by praying both prayers of the day (Lectionary 31 and All Saints) and by reading out the names of the past year’s local dead in a solemn litany of thanksgiving.

Matthew 5:1-12
The Beatitudes are appointed for All Saints because they describe a surprising grouping of persons who find their wholeness in blessings from God, rather than in the values of this world. True happiness comes from life with God. The Beatitudes proclaim trust that God will bring about salvation, despite appearances. In true biblical fashion, God is on the side of the underdog.

Revelation 7:9-17
This magnificent poetic passage describes the worship of God (“the throne”) and Christ (“the Lamb”) in the heavenly realm. Joining with angels, church leaders, and the “four living creatures” are all believers (“robed in white”) who suffered on earth. The vision means to comfort Christians who mourn their dead and to inspire trust that God will bring joy out of sorrow.

1 John 3:1-3
The reading expresses our trust that at the end, we will see God, who loves us. As children, we will see the Father; unknown by the world, we are known by God.

Images in the Readings

The main image in the gospel reading is the kingdom of heaven. The image of kingdom stresses a communal and social reality, not an individual psychic experience. This image has been so narrowed by especially nineteenth-century artists into a kind of summer camp in the skies that it is not easy to see in the image God’s transforming rule of righteousness. In Revelation, the kingdom is imagined with all the angels and saints gathered around God’s throne, a depiction common in medieval art but itself distant from our understanding of the universe.

The reading from Revelation resembles a concordance of biblical imagery, each of which connects with numerous biblical passages: every nation, throne, Lamb, palm branches, angels, the four living creatures, washed, blood, hunger, thirst, the sun’s heat, shepherd, springs of the water of life. That the saints are robed in white suggests early Christian baptismal garb, and the word “alb” refers to these white robes that clothe the baptized. In true metaphoric fashion, in which words assert the opposite of a literal meaning, the robes are made white by being washed in blood.

Connections with the Liturgy

“This is the feast,” an optional canticle of praise (ELW p. 101), quotes today’s reading from Revelation: “blessing and honor, glory and might. . . ,” and an optional call to the table (ELW p. 112) cites today’s psalm: “Taste and see that the Lord is good.”

The Prayer of the Day

The prayer of the day, inspired by one included in the 1549 Book of Common Prayer, praises God for the body of the church and asks for continued blessings. The prayer does not invoke the assistance of saints, but rather petitions for the grace to follow their lives of faith and commitment.

The Psalm for the Day

Hearing the vision of worship around the heavenly throne, we respond by singing Psalm 34:1-10, 22. The “I” of the psalm asks the entire community to join in praising God who has saved me “from all my troubles.” The Hebrew of verse 9 can be translated either as “holy ones” or “saints.” In the coming holy communion, we will taste and see the goodness of the Lord.

A Hymn for the Day

A good hymn for All Saints Day is “Holy God, we praise your name” (ELW 414). We join our praise with that of angel choirs, the apostles, the prophets, the martyrs, indeed with all the singing church, to honor the Triune God. This hymn derives from an anonymous metrical version of the classic Te Deum, perhaps originally crafted by Bishop Niceta in the fifth century.

Life on the Earth

As an indication that Christians have seen the earth itself and human creatures as good, the visions of heaven in the Book of Revelation describe not disembodied spirits floating around the skies, but resurrected human bodies who fall “on their faces” and who drink from “springs of the water of life.” This good earth is the gift God has granted to us here and now, and blessed are those who have come to honor God’s creation.

Especially for Children

Explain to children the meanings of the word “saint.” It can mean biblical characters, like St. Paul; it can refer to famous Christians over the centuries, like St. Patrick; and it also can mean every baptized person, those who have already died, and those still alive. Have each child say out loud, “I’m a saint, and so are you.”

Visual Art

From about 1700 comes an Orthodox icon that depicts the second coming of Christ, but is commonly used for All Saints Sunday. Christ is surrounded by angels and saints whose varied attire indicates their situation on earth. The lower level of the icon pictures the plantings in Paradise, in which are both Abraham enthroned and the good thief of Golgotha, holding his cross.
